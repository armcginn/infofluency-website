What the Exit Actually Looked Like: One Seller's Journey

Most people selling a business for the first time have no idea what they're walking into. Not because they're unprepared people, but because the process is genuinely unlike anything else. Here's what one seller's journey looked like, start to finish, so you have a real picture before you're in the middle of it.

The Decision Wasn't a Moment. It Was a Season.

For Marcus, the owner of a regional HVAC services company, the idea of selling started as a background hum about three years before he actually listed. He'd hit a revenue ceiling around $4.2M annually. He was working 55-hour weeks. His two key technicians were aging out. He didn't want to build the next version of the company. He wanted out.

That clarity took time. He sat with it for a year before he told anyone. Then he told his accountant, who told him his books weren't ready. That was actually useful information. It meant he had real work to do before any conversation with a buyer could go anywhere.

The honest answer to 'when should I start thinking about exit?' is usually 'earlier than you think.'

Getting the House in Order Took 18 Months.

Marcus spent a year and a half cleaning up his financials, separating personal expenses that had run through the business, documenting his service contracts, and getting two years of clean, recasted P&Ls in front of a sell-side advisor.

He also had to reckon with something uncomfortable: a lot of the business lived in his head. Customer relationships, pricing judgment, vendor negotiations. None of it was written down. Buyers price that risk into their offers. So he spent six months building out an operations manual and transitioning key customer relationships to his office manager.

None of that is glamorous. All of it mattered.

The Process Was Slower and More Human Than He Expected.

Marcus listed in late spring. He had 11 buyer conversations over four months. Most of them went nowhere, and not for dramatic reasons. A few buyers were clearly tire-kickers. One was a strategic acquirer who wanted his customer list more than his team. Two were private equity-backed roll-ups that moved fast but offered structure he didn't trust.

The buyer he ultimately chose was a first-time business owner, a former operations director in a related trade, who was putting in his own capital and a small SBA loan. Not the flashiest offer. Not the highest number. But Marcus had watched how the buyer handled every question, every delay, every document request. He was steady. He communicated clearly. He didn't play games.

The deal closed at $2.1M, roughly a 4.8x multiple on adjusted EBITDA. The earnout piece, tied to 12-month revenue retention, paid out at about 80 cents on the dollar. Marcus knew going in that earnouts almost never pay at 100%. He negotiated the base accordingly.

What He Said After It Was Done.

When we talked to Marcus about six months post-close, he said two things that stuck.

First: 'I wish I'd started cleaning up the books two years earlier. I left money on the table because I didn't know what buyers were looking at.'

Second: 'Nobody told me how emotional it would be. You spend 15 years building something and then you hand over the keys and that's it. I cried in my car the day we signed.'

Both of those things are worth knowing before you're in it. The financial preparation is learnable. The emotional weight is real regardless.

If you're somewhere in Marcus's early season, the background hum stage, that's actually the right time to start getting curious. Not to rush the decision. Just to understand what the path looks like so you can walk it with your eyes open.
