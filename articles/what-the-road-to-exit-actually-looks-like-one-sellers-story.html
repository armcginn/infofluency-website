What the Road to Exit Actually Looks Like: One Seller's Story

Most people planning a business exit picture a finish line. Sign the papers, wire the funds, done. What they don't picture is the eighteen months before that moment, and how much of it is just information work.

This is the story of one of our clients, a second-generation owner of a regional manufacturing business. We'll call him David. His exit took about two years from first conversation to close. Here's what that actually looked like.

The Starting Point Was Messier Than He Expected

David came to us thinking he had clean books. He did, mostly. But 'clean books' and 'buyer-ready data' are two different things. His financials were accurate. They just weren't organized the way a buyer's due diligence team would look at them.

Revenue was bucketed by product line internally, but buyers in his space care about customer concentration. His top three customers were 61% of revenue, and that number wasn't visible anywhere in his existing reports. It wasn't hidden. It just wasn't surfaced. That's the kind of thing that surfaces in due diligence and slows everything down, or worse, reframes the valuation conversation at the wrong moment.

We spent the first six weeks building a data room that told the story a buyer would want to read. Not spinning it. Organizing it. Customer tenure, margin by segment, revenue trend by channel. Real numbers, presented in the order that answers the questions before they're asked.

The Middle Part Is Where Most Sellers Lose Time

David had a broker he trusted. Good broker. But brokers work multiple deals at once, and the information requests that come in during a live process move fast. A buyer's team asks for something, the broker forwards it, and if the seller can't produce it quickly, the deal momentum stalls.

Over a four-month period, we fielded 47 discrete data requests on David's behalf. Some were simple pulls. Some required us to build new views from his existing data. A few required us to go back to his bookkeeper to reconstruct historical records that predated his current accounting system.

None of that is glamorous. All of it mattered. The buyer's team later told David's broker that his process was 'unusually clean.' That phrase translated directly into a shorter exclusivity period and less aggressive retrade language in the LOI.

The Last Stretch Isn't the Easy Part

There's a version of the exit story where everything after the LOI is paperwork. That wasn't David's experience, and it's usually not anyone's experience.

Final due diligence brought two rounds of follow-up questions, one of which required us to reconcile three years of inventory data across two different systems. His ERP had been migrated in year two of our lookback period, and the bridge between the old and new system had never been formally documented. We built it. It took about a week. The deal didn't fall apart, but it would have been a tense week without someone who knew where to look.

David closed in the fall. The number was in the range he'd hoped for when we started. Not every exit lands there. His did partly because the business was solid, and partly because the information told that story clearly all the way through.

What We'd Tell the Next David

Start earlier than you think you need to. Not because there's urgency, but because the cleanup work takes the time it takes. Six weeks of data organization before a process starts is a much calmer version of the same work than six days during due diligence.

Know your concentration numbers before a buyer surfaces them. Know your margin by segment. Know where your historical data has gaps, and have a plan for those gaps.

The exit is a data story. It helps to know what story your data is telling before someone else reads it out loud.

If you're starting to think about an exit and want to know what your data looks like right now, that's a good first conversation to have. We'd start with a two-week look at what you have and what it says.
