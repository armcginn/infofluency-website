Allied Materials: Live KPI Scorecard in 28 Days Post-Close

A mid-market manufacturing deal was like most, the excitement of close ran straight into the reality of the first 90 days: the new ownership group needed to see how the business was performing, and the business didn't have a clean way to show them.

Finance was closing the books in Acumatica. Ops was tracking fill rate and on-time delivery in a spreadsheet that lived on one person's laptop. Sales had its own pipeline view, updated whenever someone remembered to update it. Three departments, three versions of the truth, and a new board asking questions that needed answers in days, not weeks.

The team wasn't short on data. They were short on one place to look at it.

What had to happen in 28 days

The new ownership group set a hard deadline: a live scorecard, in front of the board, 28 days after close. No pilot, no phased rollout. One shot to show the business was under control.

Leadership knew the eight numbers that actually mattered: [e.g., revenue, gross margin, EBITDA, on-time delivery, fill rate, DSO, pipeline coverage, headcount to plan]. What they didn't have was a way to see all eight, updated automatically, without someone assembling it by hand every week.

What changed for them

By day 28, the leadership team had one dashboard, refreshed daily, that finance, ops, and sales all looked at the same way. A few things that made the difference:

The board stopped asking "can you get us that number" and started asking "what's driving that number." That's a different conversation. It's the one a new ownership group actually wants to have in month one.

Nobody was reconciling three spreadsheets to get to one answer anymore. The person who used to spend a chunk of every Monday pulling numbers together got that time back for actual analysis.

Finance, ops, and sales were finally looking at the same eight metrics, defined the same way. Before, "on-time" meant something slightly different depending on who you asked. Now it's one definition, one number, one source.

The scorecard became the agenda. Leadership meetings shifted from status updates to decisions, because everyone walked in already knowing where the business stood.

Why 28 days mattered

Post-close is when trust between new ownership and the operating team gets built or doesn't. A clean, credible scorecard delivered on time told the board that leadership could execute, not just talk about executing. That's worth more than the dashboard itself.

Where things stand now

The scorecard this team uses today started as eight numbers pulled together in a month. It's grown from there as new questions have come up, because that's how this is supposed to work: start with what you need to know now, and let it grow with you.
