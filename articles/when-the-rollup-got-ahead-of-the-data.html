When the Rollup Got Ahead of the Data

The Plan Was to Clean It Up Later

Private equity rollups move fast. That's the whole idea. You acquire three or four companies in quick succession, consolidate them onto one platform, and figure out the messy details once everyone is under the same roof.

The messy detail, in this case, was customer data.

A PE-backed rollup brought several acquired companies onto a single NetSuite instance on an aggressive timeline. Each of those companies had its own conventions for entering customer records: different naming formats, different punctuation, different ways of handling divisions and regional offices. Nobody standardized anything before the migration. The plan was to sort it out after.

That sorting never happened.

What Duplicate Records Actually Cost You

Once all those records were sitting in one system, the same real-world customer often appeared four or five times over. One entry used the full legal name. Another used an abbreviation. A third was tied to a regional office that had already been folded into a parent location. A fourth had an old AP address from before an acquisition.

NetSuite didn't know those were the same company. It just followed the records it had.

So billing followed those records too. Invoices went out to the wrong version of a customer. They landed at regional offices instead of the AP departments that actually process payments. They went to addresses that no longer existed in any operational sense.

The invoices didn't go unpaid because anyone refused to pay. They went unpaid because the right person never received them.

That distinction matters. A collections problem and a delivery problem look similar in the AR aging report. They are not the same problem, and they don't have the same fix.

The Backlog Built Faster Than Anyone Expected

Unresolved AR climbed fast. Within a few months it had reached crisis levels, and the company's cash position took the hit.

The underlying business was healthy. Revenue was real. The customers were real. The contracts were signed and the work was done. But cash wasn't coming in, and leadership was starting to have conversations that a healthy business shouldn't have to have.

This is the part that doesn't show up in rollup projections. Integration timelines account for systems and headcount. They rarely account for what happens when five companies' worth of customer naming conventions collide in one database and nobody has time to referee.

How You Start Clearing the Backlog

The fix wasn't a full data migration or a months-long cleanse project. It started with one focused question: which records in this system refer to the same real-world company?

The solution is matching to map duplicate customer records to their true parent. Fuzzy logic handles the abbreviations and punctuation differences. A little manual review handles the edge cases. The output is a clean crosswalk: this entry and that entry are the same customer, and here is the one record invoices should follow.

Once that map exists, invoices can start going where they belong. AP departments get the bills. The backlog starts clearing. Cash starts moving.

Underlying data doesn't get perfect overnight. Deduplication is a process, not a moment. But you don't need perfect to get paid. You need good enough to route the invoice correctly.

Data messes cause real problems and persist into new ERPs. Cleanup is worth the time. Real dollars are at stake.
