Board Reporting Jul 2026 2 min read

From Fire Drill to Finished Before the Board Even Asks

An aerospace distribution company, 12 hours a month spent building the board deck by hand

The situation

Board prep followed the same pattern every quarter. The days before the meeting, the CFO would pull order data from one system, sales numbers from another, and reconcile it all against whatever the management team had been looking at all month. Then reformat it into a deck the board would actually read.

The numbers were usually right. Getting there was the problem. A dropped formula, a stale tab, or a number that didn't match what the board saw last quarter could turn a routine meeting into a round of "where did this come from" instead of a conversation about the business.

Twelve hours a month of assembly work meant the days before every board meeting looked less like preparation and more like a scramble to get the numbers to agree with themselves.

What we built

We automated the reporting process so board prep pulls from the same single source the management team uses all month, refreshed automatically instead of rebuilt from scratch before every meeting.

What changed for them

The board deck stopped being a special project. The numbers the CFO needed for the board were already sitting there, current, because they were the same numbers the business had been tracking all along. No separate pull, no reconciliation pass the week before.

What the board sees now matches what management has been looking at all month. No gap between the internal view and the board view means no awkward moment explaining why this quarter's number doesn't match last quarter's definition of it.

The CFO walks into board meetings prepared instead of relieved. Prep time went from a day and a half of building to time spent actually thinking about what the numbers mean and what the board is likely to ask.

Customer order updates and the management meeting deck came along for free. Once the reporting was automated for the board, the same source fed everything else the business needed monthly, without a separate build for each one.

Why this mattered

A board meeting is one of the few times a quarter when a CFO's credibility is fully on the line. Numbers that are late, inconsistent, or hard to trace back cost more than time. They cost trust. Automating board prep meant the CFO showed up every quarter with numbers that were already right, already consistent, and already explainable, which is exactly what a board wants to see from the people running the business.
