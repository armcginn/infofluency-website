What Clean, Connected Data Actually Means for an MRO Operation

How data infrastructure transforms maintenance operations from reactive to predictive.

Ask most MRO operators whether they have data, and the answer is always yes. Work orders in the CMMS. Parts transactions in the ERP. Labor hours logged somewhere. Compliance records in a folder, a server, or both. The data exists — it's the connectivity that doesn't. In most MRO shops, these systems were never designed to talk to each other, which means the people running the operation are making decisions based on whatever they can see from where they're standing, not what's actually happening across the whole shop. McKinsey's research on MRO digitization found that up to 80% of hangar floor activity is invisible to business systems in real time. That's not a technology gap — that's a leadership information gap dressed up as one.

Clean, connected data means one thing practically: when something happens on the hangar floor, the right people know about it at the right level of detail, without having to call three people and pull a report. It means a director of maintenance can look at a single dashboard and see TAT by work package, parts availability against open work orders, labor utilization across shifts, and customer SLA risk — all at once, all current. Today, most MROs get that picture through a weekly meeting and a spreadsheet someone spent four hours building. By the time it's in front of leadership, half of it is stale.

The business outcomes of solving this aren't theoretical. MROs that have gotten their data infrastructure right are seeing 25% faster turnaround times, 30% reductions in unplanned AOG events, and 10 to 20% reductions in total maintenance spend. Those numbers move the needle fast in an industry where a single AOG event can cost an airline over $100,000, and where a missed SLA can cost you a customer relationship that took years to build. The operational improvements aren't coming from doing the work differently — they're coming from seeing the work clearly for the first time.

Parts and supply chain are where the data gap is most expensive and least visible. Most MROs discover a parts shortage when the tech is standing at the aircraft waiting. A shop with connected data can see that shortage forming two weeks before the job drops — because the demand signal from the maintenance schedule is talking to the inventory system, which is talking to procurement. That closed loop turns a reactive scramble into a planned purchase order. It also surfaces patterns: which suppliers are chronically late on which part categories, which jobs consistently blow their parts budget, which components are being over-repaired because no one is tracking consumption history across the fleet.

There's also a workforce dimension that doesn't get talked about enough. In an industry where experienced technicians are retiring faster than they can be replaced, an MRO's institutional knowledge is quietly walking out the door — encoded in the heads of people who've been doing this for 30 years, not in any system. Connected data changes that. When work history, findings, corrective actions, and inspection notes are structured and searchable rather than scattered across paper logs and legacy records, that knowledge becomes accessible to the next generation of technicians. It becomes a recommendation engine: when you see this on a CFM56, here's what the last 400 work orders tell you to check next.

None of this requires replacing the systems an MRO already has. Ramco, IFS, Quantum Control, CORRIDOR — these platforms hold enormous amounts of valuable data. What most of them lack is the analytics layer that surfaces that data as decisions rather than records. That's the gap clean, connected data solves: not more data, but data that works together, presented in a way that lets an operations leader manage their business instead of manage their spreadsheets. And for mid-market MRO operators, this is far more accessible than most assume — both in terms of implementation complexity and cost.
