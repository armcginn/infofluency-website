What McKinsey Has to Say About the Aerospace Technology Wave

Industry insights on digital adoption — and what they mean for Tier 2 and Tier 3 suppliers.

McKinsey's research on the digitization of aviation MRO (maintenance, repair, and overhaul operations) paints a picture that should be required reading well beyond the MRO hangar. The findings are specific to the MRO world, but the structural diagnosis applies across the aerospace supply chain: an industry sitting on enormous amounts of operational data, most of which never gets turned into a decision. For Tier 2 and Tier 3 manufacturers and suppliers, that diagnosis lands closer to home than most might expect.

The headline number from McKinsey's analysis is stark: only 6% of MROs have integrated digital capabilities at scale. Meanwhile, more than 80% of operators cite data limitations as their single biggest barrier to operational improvement. That's not a technology shortage — the platforms exist. Ramco, IFS, Quantum Control, and a dozen others are capturing work order data, parts transactions, and maintenance histories every day. The gap is between data that gets recorded and data that gets used. McKinsey found that up to 80% of hangar floor activity is invisible to business systems in real time, meaning the people responsible for running the operation are managing it on lag — last week's report, last month's numbers, gut feel filling in the rest.

The business case McKinsey documents for closing that gap is significant. MROs operating with mature digital and analytics capabilities are seeing 25% faster aircraft turnaround times, 30% reductions in unplanned AOG events, and 10 to 20% reductions in total maintenance spend. easyJet reduced maintenance-related flight delays by 20% on its A320 fleet. Lufthansa Technik cut TAT by a quarter. Air France-KLM, working with Google Cloud on predictive maintenance analytics, reduced analysis time from hours to minutes. These aren't pilot programs anymore — they're operational benchmarks that digitally capable competitors are already achieving. The gap between the leaders and the laggards is widening, and it's widening fast.

What makes this relevant for Tier 2 and Tier 3 suppliers is that the same dynamic McKinsey describes in MRO operations is playing out on the shop floor. A precision machining shop, a composites fabricator, an aerostructures sub-assembler — they face the same fundamental problem: operational data that lives in their ERP, their scheduling system, and their quality records, but never surfaces as a unified picture of how the business is actually performing. OTD rates, scrap and rework trends, labor utilization by work center, customer concentration by revenue — this data exists. It's just not connected, and it's certainly not in front of leadership in a form that drives action. McKinsey's research makes clear that the cost of that disconnection — in MRO — is measured in hundreds of thousands of dollars per AOG event avoided. In a Tier 2 or Tier 3 shop, it's measured in missed delivery commitments, failed audits, and valuations that don't reflect what the business is actually capable of.

The other dimension McKinsey raises that carries real weight for the supply chain is workforce. As experienced technicians and operators retire, the institutional knowledge they carry — which components fail on which platforms under which conditions, which routing steps create downstream quality problems, which customers require which accommodations — leaves with them unless it's been captured somewhere. McKinsey's framework positions connected data as the mechanism for preserving that knowledge: turning decades of free-text maintenance logs and work orders into searchable, actionable intelligence rather than an archive that no one can query. For Tier 2 and Tier 3 shops facing the same retirement cliff, the analogy is direct. The senior machinist who knows why the first op on a particular job always needs a tool offset adjustment, or the quality tech who knows which inspection step catches 70% of the non-conformances — that knowledge needs to live somewhere other than in one person's head.

McKinsey's conclusion is that digital maturity in aerospace is no longer a differentiator — it's a baseline expectation from customers, from OEM scorecards, and increasingly from the PE buyers who are acquiring supply chain companies at a pace not seen in years. The 6% of operators who have gotten there are pulling ahead. The 94% who haven't are sitting on the data they need to catch up. For Tier 2 and Tier 3 suppliers, the technology wave McKinsey is describing isn't something to wait for — it's already arriving in the form of tighter OEM audit requirements, more sophisticated buyer due diligence, and a competitive landscape where the companies with operational visibility are quoting with confidence while everyone else is still guessing. Getting connected data in front of the right people isn't a transformation project. For most shops, it's one analytics layer away.
