NSL Aerospace: One View Across Three Companies

The situation

The platform had done what it set out to do: close three add-on acquisitions in twelve months. Each one brought real capability. Each one also brought its own system, its own chart of accounts, and its own way of tracking the business.

By the time the third deal closed, the PE operating partner had three companies to look at and no way to look at them together. The platform company's own leadership team faced the same problem from the inside: they could see their piece, but not how the pieces added up.

Every board meeting meant three sets of numbers, three formats, and a fair amount of time spent just getting everyone to agree on what the numbers meant before anyone could talk about what to do about them.

What the business needed

Not a new system to replace the three. Ripping out and replacing ERPs at three newly acquired companies, right after close, would have created more disruption than the visibility problem was worth.

What leadership and the operating partner needed was one place where the combined business and each individual entity told the same story, built on the same definitions, without asking three finance teams to change how they worked day to day.

What we built

A single source of truth that sits on top of all three systems. It pulls from each entity's data, standardizes it against one set of definitions, and rolls it up two ways: the combined platform, and each entity on its own.

Same revenue growth calculation. Same margin definition. Same on-time and quote win/loss metrics, no matter which system the underlying data started in.

What changed for them

The operating partner got one view of the platform, plus a real way to compare the three entities against each other. Which one is ahead of plan, which one needs attention, and why, without three separate conversations to get there.

Each entity's leadership team kept their own system and gained a clear picture of how their business rolls up into the platform. They didn't have to learn a new ERP to get better visibility. They just started seeing their numbers in the combined context for the first time.

Board meetings shifted from reconciling numbers to reviewing progress. Nobody spent the first twenty minutes arguing about whose definition of gross margin was right. The conversation started where it should: how is the business actually doing against the plan.

Add-on four, when it comes, has a place to plug into. The single source of truth wasn't built for three entities. It was built for however many the platform grows to.

Why this mattered for a rollup

A platform strategy only works if leadership and the sponsor can actually see whether it's working. Three good acquisitions with no combined view isn't a platform yet, it's three companies that happen to share an owner. The visibility is what turns the roll-up into one business the operating partner can actually manage.
