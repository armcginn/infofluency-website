Distribution Company: From Spreadsheet Chaos to Automated Reporting

A distribution company, 12 hours a month spent building reports by hand

The situation

Every month, the CFO lost a day and a half to reporting. Pull order data from one place, sales numbers from another, stitch it all into spreadsheets, format it for the management meeting, then do a version of the same work again for the board.

None of it was hard, exactly. It was just slow, manual, and easy to get wrong. A dropped formula or a stale tab could send the wrong number into a board deck, and nobody would catch it until someone asked a question the numbers couldn't answer.

Twelve hours a month doesn't sound like much until you add up what it costs: a CFO spending a day and a half assembling numbers instead of doing the work only a CFO can do.

What we built

We automated the reporting process end to end. Customer order updates, the management meeting deck, and board prep now all pull from one place, refreshed automatically instead of rebuilt by hand every month.

What changed for them

The CFO got twelve hours a month back. Not twelve hours of busywork moved somewhere else. Twelve hours redirected to forecasting, deal support, and the kind of financial leadership a growing distribution company actually needs from its CFO.

Customer order updates stopped being a special request. What used to take a manual pull now shows up automatically, so the team can answer a customer or a salesperson's question without stopping to build a report first.

The management meeting deck builds itself. The numbers are already right because they come from one source, not from whoever assembled the spreadsheet that week.

Board prep stopped being a fire drill. The same numbers the management team uses all month are the numbers that show up in the board deck. No reconciling, no last-minute scramble to make sure everything lines up.

Why this mattered

A CFO's time is one of the most expensive resources in the business, and spreadsheet assembly is one of the lowest-value ways to spend it. Automating the reporting didn't just save time. It moved the CFO from producing numbers to using them, which is where a CFO belongs.
